[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/BreakNES_MOS_6502.jpg/330px-BreakNES_MOS_6502.jpg)](/File:BreakNES_MOS_6502.jpg)

Schematic of a MOS 6502 [[1]](#cite_note-1)

The **6502** is an 8-bit [microprocessor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microprocessor) that was designed by [Chuck Peddle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chuck_Peddle) and [Bill Mensch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Mensch) for [MOS Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MOS_Technology) in 1975. Peddle and Mensch were previously involved in designing the [Motorola](/index.php?title=Motorola&action=edit&redlink=1 "Motorola (page does not exist)") [6800](/6800 "6800"). The 6502's performance was actually competitive with other CPUs using significantly faster clocks, partly due to a simplistic state machine implemented by [combinatorial logic](/Combinatorial_Logic "Combinatorial Logic") to a greater extent than in many other designs. Therefor it was a popular processor for [dedicated chess computers](/Dedicated_Chess_Computers "Dedicated Chess Computers") as well as general purpose systems such as the [single board computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single-board_computer) [KIM-1](/KIM-1 "KIM-1"), and early home computers, notably [Apple II](/Apple_II "Apple II"), [Atari 8-bit](/Atari_8-bit "Atari 8-bit"), [Commodore PET](/Commodore_PET "Commodore PET") and [Commodore 64](/Commodore_64 "Commodore 64"), which actually uses a close derivative of the 6502, the [6510](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MOS_Technology_6510).

# Register Files

Like its precursor, the [Motorola](/index.php?title=Motorola&action=edit&redlink=1 "Motorola (page does not exist)") [6800](/6800 "6800") (but unlike [Intel](/Intel "Intel") [8080](/8080 "8080")) the 6502 has very few registers. There was one 8-bit accumulator register (A), two 8-bit index registers (X and Y), an 8-bit processor status register (P), an 8-bit [stack pointer](/Stack "Stack") (S), and a 16-bit program counter (PC).

# Endianess

Unlike [Motorola](/index.php?title=Motorola&action=edit&redlink=1 "Motorola (page does not exist)") [6800](/6800 "6800"), the 6502 is a [little-endian](/Little-endian "Little-endian") processor, concerning the [byte](/Byte "Byte")-order of 16-bit [words](/Word "Word") in memory.

# Quotes

By [Dan](/Dan_Spracklen "Dan Spracklen") and [Kathe Spracklen](/Kathe_Spracklen "Kathe Spracklen") from their *Oral History* [[2]](#cite_note-2) on the advantages of the 6502 compared to [8080](/8080 "8080")/[Z80](/Z80 "Z80") while porting [Sargon](/Sargon "Sargon"):

```
Danny Spracklen: Yes, the instruction set was a little more efficient, I think, and more- it was more, if you could say, RISC like. ...
```

```
It was a simpler instruction set, but it actually- the cycle times on the individual instructions were very fast, like one microsecond, instead of like 4 or 5 microseconds, on the Z-80.
```

```
Kathleen Spracklen: Yes. And it had the short branch. You can’t underestimate the value that the short branch offered. They had the ability- if you could branch within a one byte distance of where you were, and using it as a sign number, positive or negative, you had an extremely fast branching instruction. So if you could make loops very, very small and tight, you could really run with high, high speed.
```

# TK20

[![](/images/thumb/d/d4/TurboKit20.jpg/300px-TurboKit20.jpg)](/File:TurboKit20.jpg)

TK20 [[4]](#cite_note-4)

The TurboKit TK20 by German engineering company *Schaetzle+Bsteh* was a discrete 6502 compatible 18 MHz high speed CPU with own fast shadow memory build from more than 100 [integrated circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_circuit), dedicated for general purpose and chess computers with 6502 processor, connected via a ribbon cable to the 40 pin processor socket of the host system [[5]](#cite_note-5) [[6]](#cite_note-6) [[7]](#cite_note-7). The TK 20 was used by [Ed Schröder](/Ed_Schroder "Ed Schroder") with his dedicated [Rebel](/Rebel "Rebel") programs at the [WCCC 1986](/WCCC_1986 "WCCC 1986") and various [World Microcomputer Chess Championship](/World_Microcomputer_Chess_Championship "World Microcomputer Chess Championship"), and also by [Ulf Rathsman](/Ulf_Rathsman "Ulf Rathsman") and his [Y!](/Y! "Y!") programs. Further, the dedicated [Saitek Leonardo Turbo](/SciSys_Leonardo#TURBO "SciSys Leonardo") mini series was extended by *Schaetzle+Bsteh* with an 18 MHz integrated TurboKit [[8]](#cite_note-8).
