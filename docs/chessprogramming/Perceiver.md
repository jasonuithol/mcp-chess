**Perceiver**,  
an experimental chess program by [Michael Barenfeld](/Michael_Barenfeld "Michael Barenfeld") and [Herbert Simon](/Herbert_Simon "Herbert Simon") of the late 60s with focus on [perceptual processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perception) in [problem solving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Problem_solving). Perceiver was able to duplicate the [eye movements](/Eye_Movements "Eye Movements") of a chess expert by adhering to the simple relations of [attack and defense](/Attack_and_Defend_Maps "Attack and Defend Maps"), based on previously developed routines of the program [Mater](/Mater "Mater") for detecting those basic chess relations [[2]](#cite_note-2).

# Fixated Squares

With the simulated [fovea centralis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fovea_centralis) [fixated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixation_%28visual%29) on a [square](/Squares "Squares") of the [board](/Chessboard "Chessboard"), information is acquired [peripherally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peripheral_vision) about [pieces](/Pieces "Pieces") standing on nearby squares that [attack or defend the fixated square](/Square_Attacked_By "Square Attacked By"), or that are attacked or defended by the piece on that square. Attention is then assumed to switch to one of these nearby squares, and, unless it immediately returns to the square already fixated, causes a [saccadic movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccade) to the new square. With the fovea fixated on the new square, the process simply repeats, causing a biased random walk of the fixation point around the board, returning most frequently to those regions of interest where relations among pieces are densest and spending little time on the edges of the board.

# MAPP

[Kevin J. Gilmartin](/Kevin_J._Gilmartin "Kevin J. Gilmartin") and Herbert Simon further extended Perceiver into a system called [MAPP](/MAPP "MAPP") (Memory-aided Pattern Perceiver) which uses the [learning](/Learning "Learning") mechanism of [EPAM](/EPAM "EPAM"), and reinforced the [chunking](/Chunking "Chunking") hypothesis by subjecting MAPP to the [same board reconstruction experiment](/William_Chase#Perception "William Chase") that the human players faced [[3]](#cite_note-3) [[4]](#cite_note-4).
