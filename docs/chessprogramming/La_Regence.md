Café de la Régence in 1874 [[1]](#cite_note-1)

**La Regence**,  
a [dedicated chess computer](/Dedicated_Chess_Computers "Dedicated Chess Computers") incorporating a [Z80](/Z80 "Z80") processor with a [Cyrus](/Cyrus "Cyrus") program by [Richard Lang](/Richard_Lang "Richard Lang"), designed by [Intelligent Software](/Intelligent_Software "Intelligent Software"). In 1981, their intention after recruiting Lang was in marketing an improved version of Cyrus in a [sensory board](/Sensory_Board "Sensory Board") computer. In January 1982, [David Levy](/David_Levy "David Levy") and [Kevin O’Connell](/Kevin_O%E2%80%99Connell "Kevin O’Connell") visited the [Las Vegas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Las_Vegas,_Nevada) [Winter Consumer Electronics Show](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_Electronics_Show). There they started talking to some people who distributed chess sets in [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris) [[2]](#cite_note-2). Manufactured by a French company around [Frederic Ries](/index.php?title=Frederic_Ries&action=edit&redlink=1 "Frederic Ries (page does not exist)") [[3]](#cite_note-3), the computer was named after the famous 19th century [Café de la Régence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_de_la_R%C3%A9gence) in Paris, and La Regence went on sale in France in November 1982, a luxury machine with a magnetic sensory board for the French market, priced at 3,900 [francs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_franc).

# Tournaments

According to [Kevin O’Connell](/Kevin_O%E2%80%99Connell "Kevin O’Connell"), a La Regence program already played the [ACM 1981](/ACM_1981 "ACM 1981") Blitz tournament still running on an [Osborne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osborne_1) [Z80](/Z80 "Z80") based computer, beating [Cray Blitz](/Cray_Blitz "Cray Blitz") and [Chess 4.5](/Chess_(Program) "Chess (Program)"), and only losing from [Belle](/Belle "Belle") [[4]](#cite_note-4).
However, the [Philidor Software](/Philidor_Software "Philidor Software") program which played the ACM 1981 main tournament was called [Philidor](/Philidor "Philidor") with [David Broughton](/David_Broughton "David Broughton") and [Mark Taylor](/Mark_Taylor "Mark Taylor") mentioned as authors in the booklet [[5]](#cite_note-5) and likely more related to [Vega](/Vega "Vega") rather than [Cyrus](/Cyrus "Cyrus").
A dedicated La Regence prototype played the [European MCC 1982](/European_MCC_1982 "European MCC 1982"), and became unlucky runner up behind [Advance 2.4](/Advance "Advance") after losing in the final round by it's own stablemate Philidor.

# Sandy TSB 4 La Regence

[![](/images/thumb/4/40/TSB_IV_La_Regence.jpg/300px-TSB_IV_La_Regence.jpg)](https://www.flickr.com/photos/10261668@N05/859053238/in/album-72157600923814103/)

TSB IV La Regence [[9]](#cite_note-9)

The La Regence was a commercial flop in France - in 1983 [Ries'](/index.php?title=Frederic_Ries&action=edit&redlink=1 "Frederic Ries (page does not exist)") company went into liquidation and some of the remaining stocks could find their way to the Market [[10]](#cite_note-10), distributed by [Sandy Electronic](/index.php?title=Sandy_Electronic&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sandy Electronic (page does not exist)"), the German company in the dedicated chess computer market otherwise associated with trading [Applied Concepts](/Applied_Concepts "Applied Concepts") aka [Chafitz](/Chafitz "Chafitz") computers under their own brand.
Despite as mentioned elsewhere [[11]](#cite_note-11) [[12]](#cite_note-12), there is probably no other connection of the La Regence with Applied Concepts and Chafitz [[13]](#cite_note-13).
