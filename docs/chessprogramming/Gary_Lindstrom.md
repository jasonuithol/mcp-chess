Gary Lindstrom [[1]](#cite_note-1)

**Gary Edward Lindstrom**,  
an American mathematician, computer scientist, and [professor emeritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professors_in_the_United_States#Professor_emeritus_and_emerita) at School of Computing [[2]](#cite_note-2), [University of Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Utah). He is a graduate of [Carnegie Mellon University](/Carnegie_Mellon_University "Carnegie Mellon University"), where he earned B.S. and M.S. degrees in mathematics, and a Ph.D. in computer science in 1971 under advisor [Alan Jay Perlis](/Mathematician#Perlis "Mathematician"). Among his research interests are [data management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_management), [verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_verification), and [programming language design](/Languages "Languages"), specification and implementation [[3]](#cite_note-3). He worked on [search](/Search "Search") algorithms, in particular, like [SSS\*](/SSS*_and_Dual* "SSS* and Dual*"), the logically parallel [alpha-beta](/Alpha-Beta "Alpha-Beta") approach of *Evolving Tree Search* (ets) [[4]](#cite_note-4), and based on ets and the Mandatory Work First (mwf) approach by [Selim Akl](/Selim_Akl "Selim Akl") et al. [[5]](#cite_note-5), the [highly-parallel alpha-beta](/Parallel_Search "Parallel Search") algorithm dubbed *The Key Node Method* [[6]](#cite_note-6).
