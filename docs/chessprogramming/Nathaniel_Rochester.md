Nathaniel Rochester [[1]](#cite_note-1)

**Nathaniel Rochester**, (January 14, 1919 – June 8, 2001)  
was an American electrical engineer and pioneer in computer science and [artificial intelligence](/Artificial_Intelligence "Artificial Intelligence").
He received a B.Sc. in electrical engineering from [Massachusetts Institute of Technology](/Massachusetts_Institute_of_Technology "Massachusetts Institute of Technology") in 1941,
and moved to [IBM](/index.php?title=IBM&action=edit&redlink=1 "IBM (page does not exist)") in 1948, where he designed the [IBM 701](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM_701) and wrote the first symbolic [assembler](/Assembly "Assembly"),
which allowed programs to be written in short, readable commands rather than pure numbers or punch codes.
A group headed by Rochester simulated [neural networks](/Neural_Networks "Neural Networks") on an [IBM 704](/IBM_704 "IBM 704") computer [[2]](#cite_note-2).

In [1955](/Timeline#1955 "Timeline"), Rochester co-organized the [Dartmouth Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dartmouth_Conference) along with [John McCarthy](/John_McCarthy "John McCarthy"), [Marvin Minsky](/Marvin_Minsky "Marvin Minsky") and [Claude Shannon](/Claude_Shannon "Claude Shannon") [[3]](#cite_note-3),
and later supervised AI projects, including [Arthur Samuel's](/Arthur_Samuel "Arthur Samuel") checkers program, [Herbert Gelernter's](/Mathematician#HGelernter "Mathematician") [Euclidean Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry) [Theorem Prover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_theorem_proving) [[4]](#cite_note-4)
and [Alex Bernstein's](/Alex_Bernstein "Alex Bernstein") [chess program](/The_Bernstein_Chess_Program "The Bernstein Chess Program"). In 1958, he was a visiting professor at MIT, where he helped [John McCarthy](/John_McCarthy "John McCarthy") with the development of [Lisp](/index.php?title=Lisp&action=edit&redlink=1 "Lisp (page does not exist)") programming language.
In the 1960s, Rochester continued to work at IBM, directing cutting edge research in [cryogenics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenics) and [tunnel diode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tunnel_diode) circuits [[5]](#cite_note-5).
