**Machiavelli**,  
a chess program developed in [1947-1948](/Timeline#1948 "Timeline") by [Donald Michie](/Donald_Michie "Donald Michie") and [Shaun Wylie](/Shaun_Wylie "Shaun Wylie") which was a rival "paper machine" of [Alan Turing's](/Alan_Turing "Alan Turing") and [David Champernowne's](/David_Champernowne "David Champernowne") [Turochamp](/Turochamp "Turochamp"). Turing began programming both at [University of Manchester](/University_of_Manchester "University of Manchester") but never completed them and they never played each other [[2]](#cite_note-2).

In their 1961 article *Machines that play games* [[3]](#cite_note-3), [John Maynard Smith](/John_Maynard_Smith "John Maynard Smith") and [Donald Michie](/Donald_Michie "Donald Michie") elaborate the [evaluation](/Evaluation "Evaluation") features of their one-ply analyzers [SOMA](/SOMA "SOMA") and Machiavelli, to consider [material](/Material "Material"), [center](/Center "Center") and [neighboring king square control](/King_Safety#SquareControl "King Safety"), pieces [en-prise](/En_prise "En prise"), [swap-off values](/Static_Exchange_Evaluation "Static Exchange Evaluation") and other [tactical](/Tactics "Tactics") and [strategical](/Strategy "Strategy") considerations. Later, John Maynard Smith built a SOMA-Machiavelli hybrid named SOMAC (SOMA with features taken from the Machiavelli) [[4]](#cite_note-4). This machine, when allowed a lookahead of two, has a standard of play equal to that of a mediocre human player ... [[5]](#cite_note-5) [[6]](#cite_note-6) .

Following game between [SOMA](/SOMA "SOMA") and Machiavelli was played, with the conclusion that it seems unlikely that a one-ply analyzer would beat any but the most inexperienced human player.
