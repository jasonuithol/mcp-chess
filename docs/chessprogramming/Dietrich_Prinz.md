**Dietrich G. Prinz**, (March 29, 1903 - December 1989)  
was a German computer scientist and pioneer, who developed the first limited chess program in England [1951](/Timeline#1951 "Timeline") [[2]](#cite_note-2). The computer, a [Ferranti Mark 1](/Ferranti_Mark_1 "Ferranti Mark 1"), was not powerful enough to play a full game but could find the best move if it was only two moves away from [checkmate](/Checkmate "Checkmate"), known as the [Mate-in-two](/Mate-in-two "Mate-in-two") problem.

Dietrich Prinz was educated at [Berlin University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humboldt-Universit%C3%A4t_zu_Berlin), where his teachers included [Planck](/Mathematician#MPlanck "Mathematician") and [Einstein](/Mathematician#AEinstein "Mathematician"), and graduated with a Ph.D. in [Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy). As [Jewish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jews) scientist, Prinz escaped [Nazi-Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany) in 1938 [[3]](#cite_note-3) and settled in England [[4]](#cite_note-4). In collaboration with the [University of Manchester](/University_of_Manchester "University of Manchester"), Prinz worked as a research scientist at [Ferranti Ltd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferranti) in 1947, and became involved in the firm's work with the [Manchester Mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manchester_computers) series of computers. His interest in computer chess was likely influenced by his colleague [Alan Turing](/Alan_Turing "Alan Turing") [[5]](#cite_note-5) [[6]](#cite_note-6), and like [Michie](/Donald_Michie "Donald Michie"), [Strachey](/Christopher_Strachey "Christopher Strachey"), and others, by an important article published in 1950 by [Donald Davies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Davies), *A Theory of Chess and [Noughts and Crosses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tic-tac-toe)* [[7]](#cite_note-7) [[8]](#cite_note-8) .
