**Rook Endgame**,  
an endgame with [rooks](/Rook "Rook") and [pawns](/Pawn "Pawn") only. They are a common type of endgame in practice, occurring in about 10 percent of all games, including ones that do not reach an endgame at all [[2]](#cite_note-2). Many rook endgames have the reputation of being drawish, even with one or sometimes two pawns ahead as noted by the remark ‘*All rook endgames are drawn*’ [[3]](#cite_note-3) [[4]](#cite_note-4), which might be considered in evaluation by scaling the material balance by a factor of less than one.

# KRPKR

While most programs rely on [endgame tablebases](/Endgame_Tablebases "Endgame Tablebases") , in [rook and pawn versus rook endgame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rook_and_pawn_versus_rook_endgame), two type of positions are of particular interest whose basic features may be considered with some pattern matching to adjust the score accordantly. The [Lucena position](/index.php?title=Lucena_Position&action=edit&redlink=1 "Lucena Position (page does not exist)"), finally "building the bridge" is a win for the pawn side, and the [Philidor position](/index.php?title=Philidor_Position&action=edit&redlink=1 "Philidor Position (page does not exist)") is a draw. Most rook and pawn versus rook endgames reach either the Lucena or the Philidor position if played accurately [[5]](#cite_note-5).

# KRPPKRP

In 2013 [[6]](#cite_note-6), [John Nunn](/John_Nunn "John Nunn") applied the 7-piece [Lomonosov Tablebases](/Lomonosov_Tablebases "Lomonosov Tablebases") to R+2P vs. R+P positions from the famous book *Rook Endings*, 2nd edition, by [Levenfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigory_Levenfish) and [Smyslov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasily_Smyslov) [[7]](#cite_note-7), which was assumed to contain the truth and, owing to Nunn, this is no longer so.
