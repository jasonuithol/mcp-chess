| The **Réti Endgame Study**,  composed by [Richard Réti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_R%C3%A9ti) and published in 1921 in *[Kagan's Neueste Schachnachrichten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Kagan)* [[1]](#cite_note-1), demonstrates how a king can make multiple threats, and how it can take more than one path to a given location with the same number of moves. White to move and draw!  At the first glance it seems that White has no hope. His king is far [outside the square](/Rule_of_the_Square "Rule of the Square") of the black pawn and further has a long way from supporting his own pawn with the opponent king inside its square.  But the white king threatens to support his own [passer](/Passed_Pawn "Passed Pawn"), while simultaneously coming closer to the black passer's pawn square. While the black king will have to spend two [tempi](/Tempo "Tempo") to stop the white passer from [queening](/Promotions "Promotions"), this is the number of tempi the white king needs to gain in order to get into the square of the black passer.  Inside a chess program's [evaluation](/Evaluation "Evaluation"), a too "rough" [rule of the square](/Rule_of_the_Square "Rule of the Square") implementation with huge "winning" scores will fail badly here, despite [search](/Search "Search") with [transposition table](/Transposition_Table "Transposition Table") will solve Réti's study quickly, not to mention [tablebases](/Endgame_Tablebases "Endgame Tablebases"). | |  | | --- | |                                                                  ♔          ♚ ♙             ♟ | |
