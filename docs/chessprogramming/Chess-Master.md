Chess-Master [[1]](#cite_note-1)

**Chess-Master**,  
a [dedicated chess computer](/Dedicated_Chess_Computers "Dedicated Chess Computers") series manufactured in the 80s by [VEB Mikroelektronik „Karl Marx“ Erfurt](/VEB_Mikroelektronik_Erfurt "VEB Mikroelektronik Erfurt"), [East Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Germany), in [West Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Germany) sold by Radiophon. Chess-Master ran on an 8-bit [U880](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U880) CPU, which was an unlicensed [Z80](/Z80 "Z80") clone, and had 10 KB [ROM](/Memory#ROM "Memory") and 2 KB [RAM](/Memory#RAM "Memory"). The chess program authors were [Dieter Schultze](/Dieter_Schultze "Dieter Schultze") and [Rüdiger Worbs](/R%C3%BCdiger_Worbs "Rüdiger Worbs").

[Automatic piece recognition](/Piece_Recognition "Piece Recognition") was implemented with [Hall effect sensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hall_effect_sensor) under each square of the chessboard, while pieces had two reverse directed [permanent magnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnet) with different compensation embedded, so that a cyclic running multiplexed I/O program, also polling the keyboard, could recognize the pieces [[2]](#cite_note-2) [[3]](#cite_note-3) .

Chess-Master played the [3rd World Microcomputer Chess Championship](/WMCCC_1983 "WMCCC 1983") 1983 in [Budapest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budapest), running on a 2.5 MHz processor, searching 12 - 15 [nodes per second](/Nodes_per_Second "Nodes per Second") [[4]](#cite_note-4), and competed with [Fidelity Sensory 9](/Sensory_9 "Sensory 9") for the title of the best commercial entry [[5]](#cite_note-5). According to [Ingo Althöfer](/Ingo_Alth%C3%B6fer "Ingo Althöfer"), the Chess-Master was a clone of the [Sargon 2.5](/Chafitz_ARB_Sargon_2.5 "Chafitz ARB Sargon 2.5") [6502](/6502 "6502") program by [Dan](/Dan_Spracklen "Dan Spracklen") and [Kathe Spracklen](/Kathe_Spracklen "Kathe Spracklen") - the 6502 had to be emulated by the U880 aka Z80 which apparently resulted in that low speed [[6]](#cite_note-6) [[7]](#cite_note-7).

# Chess-Master Table

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Schachcomputertisch-CMT.jpg/330px-Schachcomputertisch-CMT.jpg)](/File:Schachcomputertisch-CMT.jpg)

Chess-Master Table [[8]](#cite_note-8) [[9]](#cite_note-9)

The Chess-Master Table was a rare piece of jewelry. Only approximately 10 issues were build for special purposes - for instance, one Chess-Master Table was dedicated to [Fidel Castro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fidel_Castro), a gift by [Erich Honecker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Honecker) [[10]](#cite_note-10) [[11]](#cite_note-11) .

# Chess-Master Diamond

[![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Schachcomputer-CM-diamond.jpg/330px-Schachcomputer-CM-diamond.jpg)](/File:Schachcomputer-CM-diamond.jpg)

Chess-Master Diamond [[12]](#cite_note-12)

The improved **Chess-Master Diamond** was contributed by [Rüdiger Worbs](/R%C3%BCdiger_Worbs "Rüdiger Worbs") and [Wolfgang Pähtz](/Wolfgang_P%C3%A4htz "Wolfgang Pähtz") [[13]](#cite_note-13) , and was released in 1987 [[14]](#cite_note-14).

# Video Chess-Master

[![](/images/8/8f/VideoChess-Master.jpg)](https://www.schachburg.de/threads/2414-Der-Video-Chess-Master-in-der-DDR)

Video Chess-Master Screen [[15]](#cite_note-15)

The Chess-Master program was further ported to the [KC 85/2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KC_85) [personal computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_computer) by [Wolfgang Pähtz](/Wolfgang_P%C3%A4htz "Wolfgang Pähtz") [[16]](#cite_note-16).
