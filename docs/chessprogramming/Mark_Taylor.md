**Mark Taylor**,  
a British computer chess programmer, in the 80s working for [David Levy's](/David_Levy "David Levy") and [Kevin O’Connell's](/Kevin_O%E2%80%99Connell "Kevin O’Connell") companies [Philidor Software](/Philidor_Software "Philidor Software") and [Intelligent Software](/Intelligent_Software "Intelligent Software"). Mark Taylor, a recruit from outside the computer chess tournament circuit, was a [6502](/6502 "6502") expert who developed his programs on a [Commodore PET](/Commodore_PET "Commodore PET"). The [SciSys](/Saitek "Saitek") [Intelligent Chess](/Intelligent_Chess "Intelligent Chess") [[2]](#cite_note-2) computer and [PetChess](/PetChess "PetChess") were mainly programmed by Mark Taylor. In an interview David Levy mentioned Mark Taylor as the most talented and genius programmer of [Intelligent Software](/Intelligent_Software "Intelligent Software") [[3]](#cite_note-3).

# 160 Nibble Challenge

Mark Taylor worked with Levy to produce a program for a [Hitachi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hitachi_Ltd.) 4-bit [CMOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CMOS) [microcontroller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcontroller). The [HD44801](/HMCS4xC "HMCS4xC") had 2K of 10-bit word [ROM](/Memory#ROM "Memory"), **160** [nibbles](/Nibble "Nibble") (80 bytes) of [RAM](/Memory#RAM "Memory"), running at 400 KHz. Levy developed a chess playing algorithm for this chip which played fully legal chess including [pawn promotion](/Promotions "Promotions"), [en passant capturing](/En_passant "En passant"), [castling](/Castling "Castling"), and even managed mate with KR v K in some versions all in 160 nibbles of RAM. A piece of work that Taylor is still rightly proud of today [[4]](#cite_note-4). The chip went into a series of small portable [SciSys](/Saitek "Saitek") machines - [Mini Chess](/Mini_Chess "Mini Chess"), [Junior Chess](/Mini_Chess#Junior "Mini Chess") and [Graduate Chess](/Mini_Chess#Graduate "Mini Chess"), all released in early 1981. The 4-bit program also initiated [Eric White's](/Eric_White "Eric White") involvement in computer chess business and long time collaboration with Levy, when [Hong Kong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hong_Kong) based manufacturer *White and Allcock*, forerunner of [Newcrest Technology](/Newcrest_Technology "Newcrest Technology") introduced the CXG brand with [CXG Sensor Computachess](/CXG_Sensor_Computachess "CXG Sensor Computachess") in 1981 [[5]](#cite_note-5) [[6]](#cite_note-6).

# Philidor

Taylor was also involved in the [Philidor](/Philidor "Philidor") project, and was responsible to translate the [Z80](/Z80 "Z80") program for the SciSys [Chess Champion Mark V](/Chess_Champion_Mark_V "Chess Champion Mark V") 6502 branch, also deployed for the [Milton Bradley Phantom](/Milton_Bradley_Phantom "Milton Bradley Phantom").

# Cyrus 68K

In 1985, Taylor started to develop [Cyrus 68K](/Cyrus_68K "Cyrus 68K") [[7]](#cite_note-7), which was commercially available as the [Sphinx](/CXG_Sphinx "CXG Sphinx"), a [dedicated chess computer](/Dedicated_Chess_Computers "Dedicated Chess Computers") under the brand name [CXG](/index.php?title=Newcrest_Technolog&action=edit&redlink=1 "Newcrest Technolog (page does not exist)"), manufactured by [Newcrest Technology Ltd.](/Newcrest_Technology "Newcrest Technology") [[8]](#cite_note-8) [[9]](#cite_note-9) [[10]](#cite_note-10).
