Alvin M. Despain [[1]](#cite_note-1)

**Alvin M. Despain**,  
an American electrical engineer, and co-founder and [CTO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chief_technology_officer) of *Acorn Technologies* until his retirement in 2008 [[2]](#cite_note-2).
He holds a Ph.D. in electrical engineering from the [University of Utah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Utah) in 1966, and has been a professor at [University of California, Berkeley](/University_of_California,_Berkeley "University of California, Berkeley"), [Stanford University](/Stanford_University "Stanford University"), [Utah State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Utah_State_University), University of Utah, and [University of Southern California](/University_of_Southern_California "University of Southern California"), where he is [professor emeritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emeritus) since 2001.
His research interests include [computer architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_architecture), [multiprocessor systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiprocessor), [logic programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic_programming), [quantum computation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computer) and [design automation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_design_automation) .

# Computer Chess

Already in the 70s, Despain designed a chess machine as described by [Ozalp Babaoglu](/Ozalp_Babaoglu "Ozalp Babaoglu") in his Master's thesis
[[3]](#cite_note-3),
but never obtained funding for full construction [[4]](#cite_note-4).
In 1990, along with [James Testa](/James_Testa "James Testa"), Despain co-authored on the [Berkeley Chess Microprocessor](/Berkeley_Chess_Microprocessor "Berkeley Chess Microprocessor") (BCM) [[5]](#cite_note-5) [[6]](#cite_note-6). The BCM was apparently used in Testa's chess entity [Zerker](/Zerker "Zerker"), a promising newcomer at [ACM 1990](/ACM_1990 "ACM 1990") as it reported 7,000,000 moves per second [[7]](#cite_note-7), roughly three times faster than [Deep Thought](/Deep_Thought "Deep Thought") at that time, but damage to the machine during shipment forced its withdrawal [[8]](#cite_note-8) [[9]](#cite_note-9), and it seems the project was later abandoned.
